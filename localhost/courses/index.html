Courses

Below is a list of courses that we recommend for master students and for students that are thinking about joining our group.

Computational Physics I (FYS3150)

An introduction to numerical methods which are used in solving problems in physics and chemistry, including solutions of differential equations, matrix operations and eigenvalue problems, interpolation and numerical integration, modeling of data and Monte Carlo methods.

      • Fall semester
      • Bachelor/beginning
      • Master thesis level (third or fourth year of study)

Read more →

Computational Physics II (FYS4411)

One-body density of electrons in a quantum dot. Just one of the many systems we simulate with computational quantum physics.

This is an advanced course on computational physics with an emphasis on quantum mechanical systems with many interacting particles. The applications and the computational methods are relevant for research problems in such diverse areas as nuclear, atomic, molecular and solid-state physics, chemistry and materials science.

  • Spring semester
  • Master or PhD thesis level (fourth or higher year of study)

Read more →

Computational Physics III (FYS4460)

A slice of an argon lattice visualized in Ovito.

The course provides an introduction to methods and problems in modern statistical physics with emphasis on algorithmic and computational methods. The applications addressed and the computational methods introduced are relevant for material science, complex systems, chemistry, solid-state, molecular-, and bio-physics.

  • Spring semester
  • Master or PhD thesis level (fourth or higher year of study)

Read more →

Computational Neuroscience (FYS388)

MRI scan of the head. Source: Wikipedia

The course provides an introduction on how the properties of neurobiological systems can be modeled mathematically. In this course you will learn how to formulate and solve simple models from computational neuroscience, and you will be able to navigate in and acquire knowledge from scientific literature in the subject field in order to be able to develop more complicated models.

  • Spring semester
  • Master or PhD thesis level (fourth or higher year of study)

Numerical Linear Algebra

Direct methods for linear equations, matrix norms and condition numbers, orthogonal transformations, eigenvalues and eigenvectors, over determined systems, singular value decomposition, iterative methods for linear systems

  • INF-MAT4350 Numerical Linear Algebra, fall semester. Beginning Master thesis level (fourth year of study)

Partial Differential Equations I

Partial differential equations, initial and boundary value problems, Representation and analysis of solutions by means of Fourier series, difference methods with stability analysis, maximum principles and energy integrals, Fourier integrals.

  • INF-MAT3360 Partial Differential Equations, spring semester. Bachelor/beginning Master thesis level (third or fourth year of study)

Partial differential equations and Sobolev spaces I

Classical theory of linear partial differential equations, the heat equation, the Laplace equation, the wave equation. Greens functions. Sobolev spaces, Poincare`s inequallities.

  • MAT-INF4300 Partial Differential Equations and Sobolev spaces I, fall semester. Master or PhD thesis level (fourth year or higher of study)

Partial differential equations and Sobolev spaces II

Modern theory for linear differential equations, elliptic, parabolic and hyperbolic. Theory for finite element methods. Some theory for non-linear equations, Hamilton-Jacobi equations, conservation laws.

  • MAT-INF4310 Partial differential equations and Sobolev spaces II, spring semester. Master or PhD thesis level (fourth year or higher of study)

Numerical Methods for Partial Differential Equations I

Discretization techniques (the element method, the finite difference method), solving large systems of equations (classical iterations, conjugated gradients, multigrid, preconditioning), application in mechanics, implementation techniques, project assignments.

  • INF5620, spring semester. Master or PhD thesis level (fourth year or higher of study)

Numerical Analysis

This course suits students at the Master or PhD level who seek an in-depth understanding of interpolation and approximation methods.

  • INF-MAT5350, spring semester. Master or PhD thesis level (fourth year or higher of study)

Modeling of fluid flow, heat transfer and solid deformation

No longer available.

  • MEK-INF4210, fall semester. Beginning Master thesis level (fourth year of study)

Scientific Computing, C/C++ programming

This course primarily gives an introduction to the programming language C++, but at the same time also provides an overview of the C programming language. Applications of the knowledge learned at the course are aimed at writing efficient code for numerical calculations and data structures for arrays, grids, fields, particles, etc. Examples given in this course match a corresponding course in Fortran programming such that similarities/differences between Fortran 77/95 and C/C++ become evident.

  • C/C++ programming, spring semester, Master thesis level (fourth year of study).

Scientific Computing, Fortran77/95 programming

This course gives an introduction to the Fortran programming language with focus on the Fortran 2003 version. Applications of the knowledge learned at the course are aimed at writing efficient code for numerical calculations and data structures for arrays, grids, fields, particles, etc.

  • Fortran77/95 programming, spring semester, Master thesis level, fourth year of study).
